Chapter 6: Tissues

Introduction

A tissue is a group of cells that have a common origin and work together to perform a specific function. Plants and animals have different types of tissues based on their needs.

Types of Plant Tissues

1. Meristematic Tissue

  • Actively dividing cells.
  • Found in growing regions: tips of roots and shoots.
  • Types:
    • Apical: Tip of root and shoot.
    • Intercalary: Base of leaves or internodes.
    • Lateral: Sides of stems; causes increase in girth.

2. Permanent Tissue

  • Formed from meristematic tissues, lose ability to divide.
  • Simple Permanent Tissues:
    • Parenchyma: Living, soft, storage.
    • Collenchyma: Living, flexible support.
    • Sclerenchyma: Dead, thick walls, hard support.
  • Complex Permanent Tissues:
    • Xylem: Transports water (tracheids, vessels, etc.).
    • Phloem: Transports food (sieve tubes, companion cells, etc.).

Types of Animal Tissues

1. Epithelial Tissue

  • Protective covering of body and organs.
  • Types:
    • Squamous (flat)
    • Cuboidal (cube-shaped)
    • Columnar (tall)
    • Ciliated (with hair-like structures)

2. Connective Tissue

  • Connects different body parts.
  • Types:
    • Blood (fluid connective tissue)
    • Bone (hard, supports body)
    • Ligament (connects bones)
    • Tendon (connects muscle to bone)
    • Cartilage (flexible)

3. Muscular Tissue

  • Responsible for movement.
  • Types:
    • Skeletal (voluntary)
    • Smooth (involuntary)
    • Cardiac (involuntary, only in heart)

4. Nervous Tissue

  • Transmits messages between body and brain.
  • Made up of neurons (nerve cells).

Conclusion

Understanding tissues helps in knowing how living organisms are structured and how their bodies perform various functions in a coordinated manner.
