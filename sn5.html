Chapter 5: The Fundamental Unit of Life

Introduction

This chapter introduces the basic unit of life – the cell – and explains its structure and function in both plant and animal organisms.

Discovery of the Cell

  • Robert Hooke discovered cells in 1665 while observing a cork slice under a microscope.
  • Anton van Leeuwenhoek observed living cells.

Cell Theory

  • Proposed by Schleiden and Schwann.
  • All living organisms are made of cells.
  • Cell is the basic structural and functional unit of life.
  • Rudolf Virchow added: All cells arise from pre-existing cells.

Types of Organisms

  • Unicellular: Made of one cell (e.g., Amoeba).
  • Multicellular: Made of many cells (e.g., humans).

Cell Structure

  • Plasma Membrane: Semi-permeable membrane controlling entry and exit of substances.
  • Cell Wall: Found only in plant cells; provides protection and shape.
  • Cytoplasm: Jelly-like fluid where all organelles are suspended.
  • Nucleus: Control center of the cell. Contains genetic material (DNA).

Cell Organelles

  • Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER): Rough ER (with ribosomes) helps in protein synthesis; Smooth ER helps in lipid synthesis.
  • Golgi Apparatus: Packaging and transport of proteins.
  • Lysosomes: Contains digestive enzymes; known as the “suicide bag”.
  • Mitochondria: Powerhouse of the cell; generates ATP.
  • Plastids: Found in plant cells; includes chloroplasts for photosynthesis.
  • Vacuoles: Storage sacs for water and nutrients; large in plant cells.

Difference between Plant and Animal Cells

  • Plant cells have a cell wall and chloroplasts; animal cells do not.
  • Plant cells have large vacuoles; animal cells have small or no vacuoles.

Conclusion

The cell is the structural and functional unit of life. Understanding cell structure and organelles is key to studying biology and life processes.
