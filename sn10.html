Chapter 10: Gravitation

Introduction

Gravitation is a natural phenomenon by which all things with mass or energy are brought toward one another. It is a fundamental force responsible for the motion of planets, stars, galaxies, and even light.

Universal Law of Gravitation

Every object in the universe attracts every other object with a force called gravitational force.

Formula: F = G × (m₁ × m₂) / r²

  • F = Gravitational force
  • G = Universal gravitational constant = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg²
  • m₁ and m₂ = masses of two objects
  • r = distance between their centers

Importance of Universal Law

  • Explains the motion of planets and satellites
  • Responsible for tides due to the moon
  • Holds the solar system together

Free Fall

When an object falls under the influence of gravitational force alone, it is said to be in free fall.

Acceleration Due to Gravity (g)

g = 9.8 m/s² on the surface of Earth.

g = G × (M / R²), where M is Earth's mass and R its radius.

Mass vs Weight

  • Mass: The quantity of matter. Constant everywhere. Unit: kg
  • Weight: The force with which the object is attracted towards the Earth. W = m × g

Thrust and Pressure

  • Thrust: Force acting perpendicular to a surface
  • Pressure: Pressure = Thrust / Area
  • SI unit: Pascal (Pa)

Buoyancy

When an object is immersed in a fluid, an upward force called buoyant force acts on it.

Archimedes’ Principle

When a body is partially or fully immersed in a fluid, it experiences an upward force equal to the weight of the displaced fluid.

Relative Density

Relative Density = Density of a substance / Density of water

It has no unit.

Conclusion

Gravitation governs the movement of celestial and terrestrial bodies. Its understanding helps us in scientific, astronomical, and daily applications like launching satellites, ship design, and measuring liquids.
