Chapter 9: Force and Laws of Motion

Introduction

Force is a push or pull that changes or tends to change the state of an object. Motion can be described through Newton’s three laws.

Balanced and Unbalanced Forces

  • Balanced Forces: Do not change the state of motion. Net force is zero.
  • Unbalanced Forces: Change the state of motion. Net force is non-zero.

Newton's First Law of Motion

An object continues to remain at rest or in uniform motion unless acted upon by an external force.

This law defines inertia — the tendency of objects to resist change in their state of motion.

Inertia and Mass

More the mass, more the inertia. Inertia is of three types:

  • Inertia of rest
  • Inertia of motion
  • Inertia of direction

Newton's Second Law of Motion

The rate of change of momentum is directly proportional to the applied force.

F = ma (Force = mass × acceleration)

This law explains how force affects motion quantitatively.

Momentum

Momentum (p) = mass × velocity. SI unit is kg·m/s.

It is a vector quantity and plays a key role in dynamics.

Newton's Third Law of Motion

To every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

This is observed in actions like walking, rowing, firing a gun etc.

Conservation of Momentum

Total momentum of an isolated system remains constant if no external force acts on it.

Mathematically: m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂

Applications

  • Rocket propulsion
  • Collisions and rebounds
  • Jumping from a boat

Conclusion

Newton’s laws provide the foundation for classical mechanics and help explain all types of motion in everyday life.
