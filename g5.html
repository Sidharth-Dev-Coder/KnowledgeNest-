Geography - Chapter 5: Natural Vegetation and Wildlife

1. Introduction

Natural vegetation refers to a plant community that has grown naturally without human aid. Wildlife refers to animals and birds living in the wild.

2. Types of Vegetation

  • Tropical Rain Forests: Found in areas with over 200 cm rainfall (e.g., Western Ghats, Andaman).
  • Tropical Deciduous Forests: Found in regions with 70–200 cm rainfall. Divided into moist and dry deciduous forests.
  • Thorn Forests: Found in dry areas of Rajasthan and Gujarat. Includes cactus, acacia, and date palms.
  • Montane Forests: Found in hilly and mountainous areas. Includes temperate and alpine vegetation.
  • Mangrove Forests: Found in coastal deltas. Most famous is the Sundarbans in West Bengal.

3. Importance of Forests

Forests are vital for ecological balance. They prevent soil erosion, maintain water cycle, and provide oxygen, fuel, timber, etc.

4. Wildlife in India

  • Animals: Elephants (Kerala, Karnataka), tigers (Madhya Pradesh), lions (Gir Forest), rhinoceros (Assam), camels (Rajasthan).
  • Birds: Peacocks, parrots, cranes, pigeons.
  • Reptiles: Crocodiles, snakes, turtles.

5. Conservation Efforts

  • Wildlife Protection Act (1972): Legal framework to protect endangered species.
  • Project Tiger and Project Elephant: Special initiatives to conserve tigers and elephants.
  • National Parks & Sanctuaries: Over 100 National Parks and 500+ Wildlife Sanctuaries in India.

6. Conclusion

India has rich biodiversity. Conservation of forests and wildlife is important for sustainable development and maintaining nature's balance.
