Chapter 3 – Poverty as a Challenge

Introduction

Poverty is a significant challenge for India. Despite progress, a large number of people still live in poverty. This chapter explores the concept, causes, and measures taken to reduce poverty in India.

Who are the Poor?

Poor people are those who lack basic necessities like food, shelter, clothing, healthcare, and education. They may live in slums, rural huts, or pavements, and often work in unorganized sectors with low and irregular income.

Indicators of Poverty

  • Hunger and malnutrition
  • Lack of access to education and health care
  • Unemployment and underemployment
  • Poor living conditions

Poverty Line

The poverty line is the minimum level of income needed to secure basic necessities. It is determined based on calorie intake and consumption expenditure.

  • Urban area: 2100 calories/day
  • Rural area: 2400 calories/day

Causes of Poverty

  • Colonial exploitation and poor economic growth post-independence
  • Unequal distribution of land and resources
  • Unemployment and low wages
  • Social exclusion and caste system

Groups Vulnerable to Poverty

  • Scheduled Castes and Tribes
  • Rural agricultural labourers
  • Urban casual labourers
  • Single women and physically handicapped

Interstate Disparities

Poverty is not uniform across India. States like Bihar, Odisha, and Uttar Pradesh have high poverty levels, whereas Kerala and Punjab have lower levels due to better infrastructure and welfare policies.

Global Poverty Scenario

Globally, poverty is highest in Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia. India has seen a significant decline in poverty, but the absolute number of poor remains high.

Anti-Poverty Measures

  • National Rural Employment Guarantee Act (NREGA): Provides 100 days of guaranteed wage employment.
  • Public Distribution System (PDS): Supplies food grains at subsidized prices.
  • Mid-Day Meal Scheme: Nutritional support in schools.
  • Skill development and self-employment schemes like SGSY, PMRY.

Conclusion

Eradication of poverty requires consistent efforts through employment generation, social welfare, and equitable distribution of income and resources. Education and empowerment are long-term solutions.
