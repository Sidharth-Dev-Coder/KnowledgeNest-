Chapter 4: Structure of the Atom

Introduction

This chapter explains how atoms are composed of subatomic particles and the different models that describe atomic structure.

Subatomic Particles

  • Electron (e⁻): Discovered by J.J. Thomson; negatively charged.
  • Proton (p⁺): Discovered by Goldstein; positively charged.
  • Neutron (n⁰): Discovered by James Chadwick; neutral in charge.

Thomson’s Model

  • Known as the “Plum Pudding Model”.
  • Atom is a positively charged sphere with electrons embedded in it.
  • Could not explain the results of later experiments like Rutherford's.

Rutherford’s Model

  • Gold foil experiment: Alpha particles were deflected, proving the existence of a nucleus.
  • Nucleus is small, dense, and positively charged at the center.
  • Electrons revolve around the nucleus.
  • Model failed to explain stability of atoms.

Bohr’s Model

  • Electrons revolve in fixed orbits called shells or energy levels.
  • Each orbit has fixed energy; electrons do not radiate energy while in orbit.

Atomic Number and Mass Number

  • Atomic Number (Z): Number of protons = number of electrons.
  • Mass Number (A): Number of protons + neutrons.

Isotopes and Isobars

  • Isotopes: Same atomic number, different mass number (e.g., ¹H, ²H, ³H).
  • Isobars: Same mass number, different atomic numbers (e.g., ⁴⁰Ar and ⁴⁰Ca).

Electron Distribution

Based on Bohr’s model: 2n² rule for shell distribution where n = shell number.

  • K shell: 2 electrons
  • L shell: 8 electrons
  • M shell: 18 electrons

Valency

The combining capacity of an atom. It is determined by the number of electrons in the outermost shell.

Conclusion

Atomic structure helps in understanding chemical behavior and bonding. This knowledge is foundational in chemistry and physics.
