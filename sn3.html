Chapter 3: Atoms and Molecules

Introduction

This chapter explains the laws that govern chemical combinations and introduces the concept of atoms and molecules, how they combine, and how their quantities are represented in chemistry.

Laws of Chemical Combination

1. Law of Conservation of Mass

Mass can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction.

2. Law of Constant Proportions

A given compound always contains the same elements in the same proportion by mass.

Atoms

  • Atoms are the smallest particles of an element that can take part in a chemical reaction.
  • They are indivisible by chemical means.
  • Each element has unique atoms (e.g., Hydrogen atom differs from Oxygen atom).

Dalton’s Atomic Theory

  • All matter is made of indivisible particles called atoms.
  • Atoms of a given element are identical in mass and properties.
  • Atoms combine in simple whole-number ratios to form compounds.
  • Atoms can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction.

Molecules

Molecules are formed when two or more atoms combine chemically.

  • Molecule of element: O₂, H₂, N₂
  • Molecule of compound: H₂O, CO₂

Atomicity

Number of atoms present in a molecule of an element.

  • Monoatomic: He, Ne
  • Diatomic: O₂, H₂
  • Polyatomic: O₃, P₄

Ions

  • Charged particles (cation - positive, anion - negative).
  • Formed by gaining or losing electrons.

Chemical Formula

Represents the composition of molecules in terms of atoms.

  • H₂O: 2 atoms of hydrogen, 1 atom of oxygen
  • NaCl: 1 sodium ion, 1 chloride ion

Mole Concept

  • 1 mole = 6.022 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro number)
  • Molar mass: mass of 1 mole of a substance

Summary

  • Atoms and molecules are fundamental building blocks of matter.
  • Mass is conserved in a reaction.
  • Chemical reactions involve rearrangement of atoms.
  • Compounds are formed in fixed ratios by mass.
  • The mole helps in quantifying chemical substances.
