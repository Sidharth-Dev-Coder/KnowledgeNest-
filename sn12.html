Chapter 12: Sound

What is Sound?

Sound is a form of energy that produces a sensation of hearing in our ears. It is produced by vibrating objects.

Production of Sound

  • Sound is produced by vibrating bodies.
  • Example: Tuning fork, string, drum etc.

Propagation of Sound

  • Sound needs a medium (solid, liquid, or gas) to travel.
  • It travels as a longitudinal wave in the form of compressions and rarefactions.
  • Cannot travel through vacuum.

Characteristics of Sound Waves

  • Amplitude: Maximum displacement of particles. Related to loudness.
  • Wavelength (λ): Distance between two compressions or rarefactions.
  • Frequency (f): Number of vibrations per second. Unit: Hertz (Hz).
  • Time Period (T): Time taken for one vibration. T = 1/f
  • Velocity (v): v = f × λ

Speed of Sound

  • Depends on the medium.
  • In air: ~ 343 m/s at room temperature.
  • Faster in solids > liquids > gases.

Reflection of Sound

  • Sound follows laws of reflection: angle of incidence = angle of reflection.
  • Echo: Reflection of sound heard after 0.1s.
  • Conditions for echo: Distance > 17.2 m (at 343 m/s)

Applications of Reflection

  • Megaphones, hearing aids, stethoscopes, soundboards in auditoriums.

Reverberation

Persistence of sound due to multiple reflections. Reduced by using soft materials like curtains, carpets etc.

Range of Hearing

  • Humans: 20 Hz – 20,000 Hz
  • Infrasonic: Less than 20 Hz
  • Ultrasonic: More than 20,000 Hz

Applications of Ultrasound

  • Medical imaging (ultrasound scan)
  • Cleaning electronic parts
  • Detecting cracks in metal blocks
  • SONAR (Sound Navigation And Ranging)

SONAR

  • Used to find depth of sea or locate objects underwater.
  • Works on principle of reflection of ultrasonic waves.
  • Distance = (Speed × Time)/2
