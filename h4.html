Introduction

This chapter deals with the lives of forest dwellers and how their world changed under British colonial rule. Forests were seen as a source of revenue, timber, and power by the British.

Role of Forests in Local Communities

Forests provided food, fuel, medicines, and materials for housing. Tribal people relied on shifting cultivation and hunting. They had deep spiritual and cultural ties with forests.

Colonial Control Over Forests

  • The British introduced forest laws that restricted access and use of forest resources.
  • Only certain forests were declared 'Reserved Forests'. Local communities were banned from traditional practices like hunting and grazing.
  • Shifting cultivation (jhum) was banned as it was seen as harmful to forests.

The Rise of Commercial Forestry

The British needed timber for shipbuilding and railway sleepers, leading to large-scale deforestation and commercial plantations.

Forest Acts and Their Impact

  • The Indian Forest Act of 1865 and its later amendments criminalized forest-based livelihoods.
  • Forest guards and officials harassed local communities.
  • People were fined and jailed for collecting wood or grazing animals.

Rebellions and Resistance

  • Many tribal communities rebelled against forest laws (e.g., Santhal, Baiga, and Bastar rebellions).
  • They demanded the right to use forests and protested exploitation by forest officials.

Forest Transformations in Other Countries

Similar changes occurred in Java (Indonesia), where the Dutch enforced strict control over forests. The Kalangs resisted but were suppressed.

Scientific Forestry

This was a new system where only selected trees were cut, and new plantations were planned. However, it destroyed biodiversity and local usage of forest products.

Conclusion

Colonial forest policies disrupted traditional lifestyles and harmed both people and nature. Forest management today must balance development with ecological and tribal rights.
