Chapter 7: Diversity in Living Organisms

Introduction

Diversity refers to the variety of living organisms present on Earth. Classification helps to identify and group organisms for better understanding and study.

Need for Classification

  • To organize and study the vast variety of organisms.
  • To understand relationships among organisms.
  • To identify newly discovered species.

Hierarchy of Classification

  • Kingdom
  • Phylum
  • Class
  • Order
  • Family
  • Genus
  • Species (smallest unit)

Five Kingdom Classification (By Whittaker)

  • Monera: Unicellular, prokaryotic (e.g., Bacteria)
  • Protista: Unicellular, eukaryotic (e.g., Amoeba, Paramecium)
  • Fungi: Heterotrophic, decomposers (e.g., Yeast, Mushroom)
  • Plantae: Multicellular autotrophs (e.g., Trees, Algae)
  • Animalia: Multicellular heterotrophs (e.g., Humans, Dogs)

Plant Kingdom

  • Thallophyta: Simple, no roots/stems/leaves (e.g., Algae)
  • Bryophyta: Mosses and liverworts; no vascular tissues
  • Pteridophyta: Have vascular tissues (e.g., Ferns)
  • Gymnosperms: Naked seeds (e.g., Pine)
  • Angiosperms: Flowering plants with enclosed seeds

Animal Kingdom

  • Porifera: Pore-bearing, e.g., Sponges
  • Coelenterata: Body cavity, e.g., Hydra, Jellyfish
  • Platyhelminthes: Flatworms, e.g., Planaria
  • Nematoda: Roundworms
  • Annelida: Segmented body, e.g., Earthworm
  • Arthropoda: Jointed legs, e.g., Insects
  • Mollusca: Soft body, e.g., Snail
  • Echinodermata: Spiny skin, e.g., Starfish
  • Chordata: Notochord present (includes vertebrates)

Binomial Nomenclature

  • Scientific names consist of two parts: Genus and species.
  • Example: Homo sapiens
  • Given by Carolus Linnaeus.

Conclusion

Classification helps us organize the rich diversity of life and understand the evolutionary relationships among species.
